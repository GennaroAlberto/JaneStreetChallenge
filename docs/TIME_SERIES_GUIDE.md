# A Field Guide to Tabular Time-Series Competitions

*Everything in this guide is illustrated with concrete numbers from the Jane
Street Real-Time Market Data Forecasting challenge — this repo's experiments,
the 8th-place (Volkova) solution we replicated, the #555562 reverse-engineering
discussion, and the DRW crypto 1st-place recipe we ported. Where a claim comes
from a specific experiment, the artifact path is given so you can check it.*

---

## 0. Know what game you are playing

Before any modeling, estimate the **noise ceiling** — the R² a perfect model
could achieve. In financial forecasting it is shockingly low, and it changes
every decision downstream.

In this challenge the target (`responder_6`) turned out to be a **forward
moving average of near-white noise** (see §1). The best private-LB score in
the world was **0.0139**. Not 0.9, not 0.5 — one point four percent of the
variance. Our best local single model explains 0.9%; our best ensemble 1.04%.

Why this matters — at R² ≈ 0.01:

* **Variance reduction beats model capacity.** Averaging three mediocre,
  *decorrelated* models (+0.0059, +0.0089, +0.0092) gave +0.0104 — more than
  any architecture change ever did. Meanwhile doubling the LSTM's width did
  nothing. When signal is tiny, your enemy is estimation variance, not bias.
* **Your validation score is itself noisy.** A 100-day validation tail gives
  R² estimates with noise of a few 1e-4. Differences smaller than that are
  not results; they are dice rolls. Run multiple seeds before believing a
  +0.0002.
* **Overfitting is the default outcome.** Any model flexible enough to be
  interesting can memorize noise that looks like 1% signal. Everything in
  this guide — leakage discipline, purged CV, early stopping, simple
  ensembles — is a defense against this single failure mode.

**Rule of thumb: first ask "how much is knowable?", then ask "how do I get
90% of that cheaply?", and only then ask "what architecture?"**

## 1. Reverse-engineer the data before you model it

Anonymized competition data is *generated by a process*, and the process
leaves fingerprints. A weekend of forensics is worth more than a month of
hyperparameter search, because it tells you what is predictable at all.

### 1.1 The autocorrelation function (ACF) is a structure detector

The single most productive plot in this competition (John Payne, #555562):
the ACF of `responder_6` **decays linearly and cuts off exactly at lag 20**.
That triangular ramp is the unmistakable signature of a **simple moving
average over white noise**: an SMA-w of iid noise has ACF `(1 − k/w)` for
k < w, and 0 after. Responders 7 and 8 cut off at 120 and 4 → they are
SMA-120 and SMA-4 of the same signal. Responders 3/4/5 mirror them (a second
exchange), and 0/1/2 are the differences.

We ran the same detector over the **79 features** (`scripts/
profile_features.py`) and found `feature_12/67/70` have the same triangular
ramp with cutoff ≈ 160 (ramp linearity R² = 0.98): *the features themselves
contain moving averages*, and can be deconvolved into their fresh increments
(§3.3).

**Trick:** compute per-series ACFs with FFT (`irfft(|rfft(x)|²)`), average
across series, and look for (a) sharp cutoffs → MA structure, (b) linear
ramps → SMA specifically, (c) slow geometric decay → AR/level series, (d)
spikes at fixed lags → seasonality.

### 1.2 Fourier analysis confirms and refines

The Fourier transform of an SMA-20-of-noise has a characteristic
`sin(20πf)/(20πf)` envelope. Payne matched the target's spectrum to it and
noticed the troughs were *not* sharp → **additive noise on top of the MA**.
That detail matters: it means even a perfect reconstruction of the underlying
signal cannot reach R² = 1 on the responder, and it tells you the deconvolution
(§3.3) must be regularized.

### 1.3 Cross-correlation at all lags: the lead–lag map

For each feature, compute `corr(feature(t), signal(t+k))` for k ∈ [−48, +48]
(FFT again — never loop over lags). This produces the most decision-relevant
artifact of our whole project, the **feature atlas**
(`artifacts/feature_atlas/`, `docs/FEATURE_RESEARCH.md`):

* **Lead features** — correlate with the *future* signal. `feature_37/38`:
  corr −0.067 with the next-20-step signal, peaking at k = +3. This is real,
  bankable alpha.
* **Nowcast features** — correlate with the *just-realized* signal.
  `feature_60`: corr −0.45 at k = −3. These describe the past. They are not
  useless (they feed reversal logic) but they are not alpha by themselves.
* A conventional univariate screen `corr(feature, target)` **cannot tell
  these apart**, because the target overlaps both bands. This is why naive
  feature selection misleads (§4.4).

**Trick — check stability before you believe anything:** we recomputed the
atlas on two windows 600 trading days apart. Spearman rank correlation of the
alpha scores: **+0.97**. That stability is what makes the finding usable. We
also clustered symbols by responder co-movement — looked great in-window,
Adjusted Rand Index across windows: **−0.07** (pure chance). One diagnostic,
two opposite verdicts, and both were decided by the *stability check*, not by
the in-sample strength.

### 1.4 Timing conventions: is the target shifted?

Payne found features at time t predict `responder_6` at t−20 with linear
R² ≈ 0.5 — a hundred times better than they predict it at t. Conclusion: the
stored responder at row t is actually the forward window (t, t+20]. Knowing
the exact timing convention determines (a) what "realized" means, (b) which
row of yesterday's lags aligns with which moment today, (c) how to build
aux targets (§5.4). Always pin this down empirically: regress the target at
various shifts on the features and look for the R² spike.

## 2. Data handling: leakage is the boss fight

At R² = 0.01, a leak of 0.1% future information *doubles* your validation
score and is invisible until the private LB. Treat leakage prevention as an
engineering discipline, not a checklist item.

### 2.1 Classify every feature by information timing

Three legal classes:

1. **Trailing** — uses data strictly before t (rolling means, lags).
2. **Contemporaneous** — uses data delivered *at* t (the market average of
   features across symbols at (date, time) is legal here because the
   inference gateway hands you all symbols' rows for a time_id at once).
3. **Forward** — uses data after t. Only legal as a **training target**,
   never as an input.

Volkova's engineered set is a masterclass: per-symbol rolling mean/std with
window 1000 (**trailing**), market average per (date,time)
(**contemporaneous**, verified deliverable), synthetic aux responders with
`shift(-k)` (**forward, targets only**).

### 2.2 Respect the deployment information set

This competition's gateway streams one `time_id` at a time and provides the
target's history only as **yesterday's full responder path** (lags.parquet) —
never within-day. Two consequences:

* Any within-day feature may only look backward within today.
* The only usable target history is at daily granularity. This constraint
  *shapes architecture*: it is exactly why TimeXer's endogenous/exogenous
  split (yesterday's path = endogenous, today's features = exogenous) is a
  natural fit, and why our implementation inverts its cross-attention bridge
  (today's causal stream queries yesterday's fully-known tokens — see
  `src/janestreet/models/timexer.py` docstring).

**Trick:** implement lag features as a *self-join on (symbol, date+1, time)*
rather than a shift — it is order-independent and structurally cannot reach
forward. (`FeatureBuilder._add_lagged_responders`.)

### 2.3 Freeze statistics at the leakage boundary

Standardization/clipping stats must be fit on **training dates only**;
validation is transformed with frozen stats; at inference the scaler may
`partial_fit` on **past** days only, mirroring what you could really do live.
Fitting a scaler on all data is the most common silent leak in public
notebooks: the validation mean/std carries future distribution information.

### 2.4 Embargo between train and validation

Our synthetic aux targets reach forward up to 40 steps; a 1-date embargo
(one day = 968 steps ≫ 40) removes any cross-boundary reach. The DRW winner
used a *purged group time-series split* with a one-group gap for the same
reason. General rule: **embargo ≥ the longest forward horizon anything in
your pipeline touches** (targets, labels, rolling windows).

### 2.5 Validation design

* **Walk-forward with online updates** if the competition allows online
  learning (this one effectively does, via daily lags). Our eval loop
  re-fits on day d−1 before predicting day d — scoring a *process*, not a
  frozen model. A static score and an online score are different quantities;
  report both (`lstm_modelr`: +0.0064 static vs +0.0092 online).
* **Expect CV ≠ LB in level, demand correlation in direction.** The DRW
  winner said his CV was far from the LB in absolute value, but movements
  correlated — that is all you need. Verify the correlation with a few probe
  submissions before trusting anything.
* **Fold-consistency as a selection principle** (DRW): a feature/decision
  that only wins in one fold is noise. He kept features that appeared in the
  SHAP top-20 of *every* fold. We ported this (§4.4).

### 2.6 Memory engineering is competitiveness

34.8M rows × 150 features does not fit a 16 GB laptop in float64. What works:

* **Float16 storage, float32 compute.** Roundtrip error ~1e-4 for
  standardized features — far below the noise floor. Halves everything.
* **Precompute once, memmap forever** (`scripts/precompute_dataset.py`):
  feature-engineer and standardize the entire pool one time, write a float16
  memmap + per-date row manifest; every downstream trainer streams date
  slices from it. This is also what makes *temporal bagging* (§6.3) cheap:
  many models over different date subsets of one array.
* **Sort rows so (symbol, date) blocks are contiguous** — sequence models
  reshape to (symbols, time, features) per day with zero copying.

## 3. To smooth or not to smooth

The instinct "the data is noisy, let me smooth it" is usually wrong here, and
understanding *why* is instructive.

### 3.1 The target is already smoothed — that's the problem

`responder_6` IS a moving average. The smoothing is why prediction is nearly
impossible: an SMA of white noise has expectation ≈ 0 given everything before
the window starts (`E[future noise] = 0`). Smoothing was applied by the
*organizers* precisely to pool 20 steps of unpredictable innovations into one
number. Smoothing your *predictions* or your *target* further adds nothing —
the metric compares you to the raw target.

### 3.2 Smoothing inputs: separate level from innovation

Smoothing a feature is a **low-pass filter**: it keeps the slow component and
deletes the recent increment. Whether that helps depends entirely on where
the feature's information lives:

* For **context/level** information, smoothing is fine and rolling stats are
  the classic move — Volkova's `feature − rolling_mean_1000` explicitly
  splits each feature into a slow level (removed) and a deviation (kept).
  Note the direction: she keeps the *deviation*, i.e. the high-pass part!
* For **timing/alpha** information, smoothing is destructive. Our atlas
  found the lead features (`feature_37/38`) peak at k = +3 — a 20-step
  smooth would average their 3-step-ahead information with 17 steps of
  stale context and bury the signal.
* The observed features `feature_12/67/70` arrive **pre-smoothed**
  (SMA-160 fingerprint). The useful transform is the *opposite* of
  smoothing: differencing/deconvolving to recover the fresh increment.

**Rule: decompose every series into slow level + fast innovation, hand the
model both, and never let a smoother eat a timing signal.** When in doubt,
check the feature's lead–lag curve first (§1.3): smoothing shifts the peak
left and shrinks it.

### 3.3 Deconvolution: the anti-smoothing trick

If a series is known to be SMA-w of something (ACF ramp, §1.1), you can
invert the filter. The SMA is a linear operator `r = A s + noise`; the
ridge-regularized inverse `ŝ = (AᵀA + λI)⁻¹Aᵀ r` is one precomputed
matrix multiply for all series. We deconvolved `responder_8` (SMA-4, the
sharpest window) into an estimate of the per-minute underlying signal and
validated it: the reconstruction correlates 0.85 with r8 at the right lag and
reproduces every structural prediction of #555562. This recovered signal is
what made the lead–lag atlas possible, and yesterday's deconvolved path is a
denoised, higher-resolution version of the raw lag features. λ is set by the
known additive noise (§1.2) — pure inversion would amplify it.

## 4. Feature engineering that actually moved the needle

Measured lifts on the same XGB harness (train 1399–1598, validate 1599–1698,
base R² +0.00591; `artifacts/bench/`):

| feature idea | lift | lesson |
|---|---:|---|
| raw previous-day responder lags (9 cols) | −0.00003 | raw lags are *levels*; trees can't difference them |
| multi-scale momentum + venue spread from lags (rsig) | +0.00030 | the *differences* carry the signal (SMA4−SMA20 = momentum) |
| symbol one-hot (39 cols) | −0.00002 | identity is already removed by per-symbol normalization |
| stable feature-profile cluster id (6 clusters) | +0.00022 | coarse, *temporally stable* grouping helps a little |
| DRW symbolic combos (40, pure SHAP seeding) | +0.00039 | see §4.5 |
| DRW combos, atlas-seeded (50) | **+0.00052** | seeding the search with domain structure beats pure importance |

### 4.1 Cross-sectional features

A panel (many symbols per timestamp) offers a whole axis single-series data
lacks: the **market average** per (date, time) — separating "the market
moved" from "this symbol moved" — and cross-sectional **ranks**, robust
to per-symbol scale. Market-wide features exist natively too (our atlas
found `feature_04/05/06/07` are ~93% market-common).

### 4.2 Multi-scale differences (momentum/spread)

When several observables are the *same* quantity at different smoothing
scales (here: lagged responders = SMA-4/20/120 of one signal, on two
exchanges), their pairwise differences are canonical trend/spread signals:
`SMA4 − SMA20` = short momentum, `SMA20 − SMA120` = medium momentum,
`A − B` at matching scale = venue spread. This is the rsig block: +0.0003
from five columns — while the nine raw columns they're built from gave zero.
*Structure knowledge converts useless features into useful ones.*

### 4.3 Aux targets: multi-task learning as regularization

Volkova's single best structural idea: her GRU has **four branches, each
predicting a different auxiliary responder**, combined linearly into the main
prediction. Aux targets (other responders; synthetic forward extensions like
`r6 + r6.shift(−20) + r6.shift(−40)`) are legal because targets may look
forward. Why it works at R² = 0.01: the main task's gradient is nearly pure
noise; correlated aux tasks with higher SNR shape the shared representation.
Our extension (this session): **realized/nowcast heads** — predicting the
*just-completed* SMA (`responder_6.shift(+20)`) is an R² ≈ 0.5 task, the
highest-SNR supervision available anywhere in the data (`--realized-aux`).

### 4.4 Feature selection done right (the DRW recipe)

The DRW 1st place ran, in order: (1) **correlation clustering** of features
(distance 1−|ρ|, threshold 0.6), keep each cluster's **medoid** (the member
with max Σ|ρ| to its cluster — "the most expensive point to replicate");
(2) **SHAP-consistency**: XGB per CV fold, keep features in every fold's
top-20 — robustly important, not fold-lucky; (3) symbolic recombination
(§4.5); (4) *recycling*: re-add dropped features one at a time — features
lose predictive power over time, and re-testing catches unstable ones.

Two blind spots we hit when porting it (`scripts/drw_feature_lab.py`):

* On an **already-curated** feature set, medoid pruning *hurt* (−0.00015):
  redundancy was informative. Pruning pays on raw 890-feature dumps, not on
  engineered pools. Keep all, *add* combos.
* Both SHAP and correlation screens **miss interaction-only features**: the
  raw lag columns never survive (zero univariate value) even though their
  differences are worth +0.0003. Selection must be *seeded* with structural
  candidates, not run purely on importance.

### 4.5 Symbolic feature search

Generate 2nd-order combinations of a core set — `x−y, x·y, x/y, min(x,y),
max(x,y)` — score by |corr with target| on train, keep those beating both
parents, cap mutual redundancy, validate on the untouched tail. Findings
here: +0.00052, and **37 of 40 winning combos were min/max gates** (e.g.
`min(market_factor, feature_36)`) — *conditional* structure that depth-6
trees demonstrably don't manufacture on their own. The DRW winner
additionally fed the final features through a small **autoencoder** and used
the 8-dim bottleneck as extra features (his single biggest boost — untried
here yet).

## 5. The architectures, honestly

For each: the idea, the inductive bias, what it did here, and its failure
modes. Local scores are online walk-forward weighted R² on the same tail.

### 5.1 Gradient-boosted trees (XGB) — the baseline that refuses to die

Fit thousands of shallow trees on rows as iid tabular data, time ignored
except through features. **Always build this first**: it is the fastest path
to "is there any signal", it ranks features (SHAP), and it ensembles
beautifully with neural nets because its errors are structurally different.
Here: +0.0068 static — beating several neural architectures — and it is a
permanent member of the best blend. Weaknesses: no sequence memory (temporal
context must be hand-fed as features), no cheap online adaptation, and blind
spots for smooth interactions (§4.5). Practical notes: `tree_method=hist`,
early stopping on the *real* metric with sample weights, and single-thread on
macOS if libomp is doubled up via torch (real deadlock we hit).

### 5.2 MLP — stronger than its reputation

Rows in, prediction out, no temporal anything. The DRW winner's *protagonist*
was a 3-layer MLP over ~40 curated features — evidence that **feature quality
beats architecture** when the features already encode the dynamics. Two of
his tricks worth stealing: train with **0.6·MSE + 0.4·Pearson** (at low SNR,
MSE is dominated by irreducible noise; the correlation term keeps gradient
pointed at *ranking* quality), and prefer **SGD over Adam** for
generalization on noisy objectives. Here a plain MLP on the full feature set
scored −0.0026 (worse than predicting zero) — same architecture family, no
curated features, opposite outcome. The pair of results is the whole lesson.

### 5.3 GRU/LSTM over the day — the workhorse (best single model)

Process each day's (symbols × 968 × features) as sequences; hidden state =
learned intraday context; predict every step. Inherently causal, cheap, and —
decisively here — **the best substrate for online refit** (§7.3). Volkova's
`ModelR` variant adds the 4 aux branches (§4.3). Best single model:
lstm_modelr, **+0.0092 online** (+0.0064 static). Notes: batch = one full
day (natural batching by regime); LSTM edged GRU on every comparison during
online adaptation (the forget gate earns its parameters when the model must
keep rewriting its state); width scaling did nothing (capacity is not the
binding constraint — data and noise are).

### 5.4 Causal Transformer — attention over time

Same layout as the RNN, triangular mask for causality. Here: +0.0050 online —
fine statically, but it **barely gains from online refit** (+0.0003 vs the
RNN's +0.003–0.005), which is the lever that matters in this competition. At
these SNRs attention is also more sample-hungry than recurrence. It earns a
ticket only if it decorrelates the ensemble (ours didn't — weight 0 from the
blender).

### 5.5 iTransformer — attention over *variates*

Inversion of the token axis: instead of timesteps attending to timesteps,
**features attend to features**, modeling cross-variate structure explicitly.
The published version embeds whole windows per variate token — **non-causal**
in a streaming setting, so our adaptation (`itransformer.py`) applies the
inverted attention *per timestep* (legal: nothing at time t sees t+1) and
mixes time separately with a causal block. Its natural prey is exactly the
min/max-gate structure the symbolic search found (§4.5). Where variate
attention is quadratic in features, feed it a *selected subset* (we use the
atlas lead + reversal sets) rather than all 130 columns.

### 5.6 TimeXer — endogenous/exogenous split

Design for "one target series + many covariates": the target's own history is
patchified into tokens (endogenous), covariates get variate tokens
(exogenous), and a global token bridges the two via cross-attention. The
mapping to this challenge is unusually clean — **endogenous = yesterday's
full responder path** (the daily lags: fully known at day start, may be
attended non-causally), **exogenous = today's streaming features** (must stay
causal) — but it forces one inversion of the paper: our *causal today-stream
queries the fully-known yesterday-tokens*, not the reverse
(`timexer.py` docstring). Verdict here: still unproven — one interrupted
CPU run; the synthetic bench (§8) exists largely to decide whether it
deserves the GPU time.

### 5.7 Path signatures — beautiful, redundant

Signatures summarize a path by its iterated integrals (with a Volterra/Hurst
reweighting for rough-kernel memory); theoretically a universal feature map
for sequential data. Everything worked technically (AR-based channel
selection beat correlation selection; the Lyndon log-signature basis was
efficient) and the best variant reached +0.0062 — yet added **zero** to the
ensemble: the GRU's hidden state already encodes the same intraday geometry,
so the streams were too correlated. Kept as theory (`janestreet/theory/`),
dropped from production. *Elegance is not additivity.*

### 5.8 Choosing among them

The question is never "which is best" but **"which set has decorrelated
errors"**. Concretely: probe each architecture cheaply (2 seeds, small
config), compute pairwise correlation of validation *prediction streams*
against your incumbents, and only scale up architectures that are both
non-negative in score and < ~0.9 correlated with what you have. Diversity
comes from inputs and data slices at least as much as from architectures
(§6.3) — and remember the strongest single lever here was not an architecture
at all but online refit (§7.3).

## 6. Ensembling

### 6.1 Simple average is the champion until proven otherwise

Every weight-optimized blend we fit on a held-out half **lost** to the simple
average on the other half — optimized weights kept collapsing into corner
solutions (all-XGB, or zero on whole families). Weight fitting is itself a
low-SNR estimation problem; with 3–6 members you do not have the data to
solve it. Non-linear stacking (XGB on top of streams) also lost. DRW's winner:
same story — NN protagonist, XGB "just to provide small improvement", simple
combination.

### 6.2 Diversity accounting

Our winning 3-way blend: xgb (static, trees) + gru_modelr (online) +
lstm_modelr (online) → +0.0104 from members {+0.0068, +0.0089, +0.0092}.
Adding a 4th member that scored a respectable +0.0062 (sig-transformer)
*lowered* the blend to +0.0101 — it was correlated with the RNNs, so it
diluted rather than diversified. **Score admission to the ensemble on
decorrelation, not on solo score.**

### 6.3 Cheap diversity: seeds and temporal bags

Two knobs that need no new architecture: **seeds** (Volkova averaged 16 seeds
of one architecture) and **temporal bagging** — many members trained on
different (overlapping) date subsets from the same precomputed memmap. Both
attack estimation variance directly, which per §0 is the dominant error term.

## 7. Monitoring training: what "learning" looks like at R² = 0.01

### 7.1 Recalibrate your eyes

Numbers that look broken but are actually *fine* here:

* **Training loss ~0.97–1.02 forever.** The weighted-R² loss is essentially
  `1 − R²`; a perfect model plateaus at ≈ 0.99. Watch the third decimal.
* **Train R² negative in epoch 1** (e.g. −0.02): the model starts worse than
  predicting zero (which is what the metric's denominator implies). It
  should cross 0 and creep to +0.005–0.02. Much above train R² ≈ 0.03 with
  validation flat = memorizing noise; tighten regularization.
* **Validation R² of +0.008 is a good model.** A +0.05 validation score in
  this problem is not brilliance; it is a leak. Go hunt it (§2).

Early stopping: patience ~10 epochs on validation weighted-R², restore-best.
The val curve is noisy enough that patience 2–3 fires on fluctuations.

### 7.2 The debugging ladder

When a model underperforms, in order:

1. **Score "predict zero" and a 10-feature linear probe** — anchors the
   floor and the sanity band.
2. **Overfit 3 days on purpose** — if it can't reach train R² 0.2 on a tiny
   slice, the wiring is broken (shapes, masks, loss), not the hyperparams.
3. **Causality audit** — shift each suspicious feature +1 step; score
   *collapsing* means it was leaking. Also compare "same inputs, shuffled
   within-day" — if the score survives shuffling, your model ignores time.
4. **Seed variance** — 3 seeds; if between-seed spread ≥ the effect you're
   studying, the effect isn't real yet.
5. Only then: hyperparameters.

### 7.3 Online refit — the biggest lever, and per-family LR cliffs

Where the environment drifts (markets!), a **daily gradient step on
yesterday's labels** before predicting today beats any static model. Here it
was worth **+0.003–0.005** to RNNs — more than every architecture innovation
combined. But the refit learning rate is *architecture-family-specific*, and
getting it wrong doesn't just underperform — it destroys:

| family | viable refit LR | optimum | at RNN's default (3e-4) |
|---|---|---|---|
| GRU/LSTM | 1e-4 – 3e-3 | 1e-3 | fine |
| signature-transformer | 1e-5 – 1e-4 | 3e-5 | **−0.0015 (regression!)** |

For months the logs said "transformers regress under online refit" and it
looked architectural. It was a **10× LR mismatch** (at 1e-3 the
sig-transformer's online score was −0.009 — catastrophic). *Sweep refit-LR
per family before concluding anything about online behavior.* Also note our
`update()` rebuilds the optimizer each day, so refit runs without momentum
state — one clean step at the right LR, no Adam moments to poison.

### 7.4 Data recency: sweep the window, expect a knee

Old data can be worse than no data under drift. Window sweep (same GRU):
50 days → +0.003, 100 → +0.005, 200 → +0.006, 400 → +0.007 (online), while a
curriculum warm-started out to 800 days **lost** to the 280-day baseline
(+0.0077 vs +0.0089), and old-data static R² on the recent tail is negative.
Train on a recent window; let online refit handle the very latest drift.

## 8. Synthetic benchmarking: triage architectures before buying GPU time

When you know (or have reverse-engineered) the DGP shape, you can build a
**controlled world with planted mechanisms and a computable ceiling**
(`scripts/synth_bench.py`) instead of burning GPU-days per architecture:

1. Simulate the generative process at the *real problem's SNR* — this is the
   part most synthetic tests get wrong; an architecture that wins at R² = 0.5
   routinely loses at R² = 0.01 where regularization dominates.
2. Plant one mechanism per inductive bias — mean-reversion (any temporal
   model), yesterday-trend × time-of-day (TimeXer's endo-patch mechanism),
   min-gates between persistent factors (iTransformer's variate attention),
   noisy future-peeking features (anything). Keep each toggleable.
3. Write a closed-form **reference predictor** per mechanism; report every
   model as *% of reference*, not raw R².
4. Run everything through the **identical production protocol** (same
   feature pipeline, frozen scalers, day-by-day online refit) with
   **identical inputs**, so a win is attributable to the architecture and
   nothing else.

Read the result as mechanism attribution: if TimeXer ≈ GRU on a world
*designed* for TimeXer's bias at realistic data volume, it has no chance on
the real data — decision made for the cost of a laptop-hour. If it does
capture its mechanism, regenerate with that mechanism zeroed and diff.

## 9. The condensed checklist

**Before modeling**
- [ ] Estimate the noise ceiling; set expectations off the leaderboard.
- [ ] ACF/FFT every target and feature; look for MA ramps, cutoffs, shifts.
- [ ] Lead–lag map features vs (deconvolved) target; split lead from nowcast.
- [ ] Verify any structure on two distant windows before using it.

**Data**
- [ ] Classify every feature: trailing / contemporaneous / forward.
- [ ] Frozen preprocessing stats at the train boundary; embargo ≥ max horizon.
- [ ] Walk-forward validation with the deployment information set (daily lags
      only, streaming causality).
- [ ] Float16 storage + precomputed memmap; contiguous (symbol, date) blocks.

**Features**
- [ ] Split level vs innovation; never smooth away a timing signal;
      deconvolve pre-smoothed series.
- [ ] Cross-sectional: market means, ranks; multi-scale differences of
      same-quantity-different-window observables.
- [ ] Aux targets (forward *and* realized) for multi-task shaping.
- [ ] Selection: fold-consistent SHAP, but seed with structural candidates;
      try symbolic min/max/product combos; consider an AE bottleneck.

**Models**
- [ ] XGB first, always. Then an RNN with aux heads. Attention only with a
      specific mechanism hypothesis (and synthetic triage if unsure).
- [ ] Judge candidates by ensemble decorrelation, not solo score.

**Training**
- [ ] Loss ≈ 1 − weighted R²; watch third decimals; patience ~10.
- [ ] Debug ladder: zero-baseline → tiny-slice overfit → causality shuffle →
      seeds → only then hyperparams.
- [ ] Online refit on, and refit-LR swept *per architecture family*.
- [ ] Recency window sweep; don't train on ancient regimes.

**Ensembling**
- [ ] Simple average; seeds and temporal bags before new architectures;
      admit members by decorrelation.
